SAIR: The Foundation for Science and AI Research

Co-founded by Fields Medalist Terence Tao, SAIR unites Nobel, Turing, and Fields laureates to scale scientific discovery with AI and ground AI in scientific principles. Launched February 2026 with a public kickoff at UCLA's IPAM, the foundation addresses a simple question: what happens when the world's best scientists and the world's most powerful AI tools work together?

Why SAIR exists: the gaps in the research ecosystem

Problem: science is under-equipped for the AI era

SAIR was born from a recognition that the current research ecosystem has critical gaps that prevent science from fully leveraging AI — and prevent AI from being grounded in scientific rigor.

Funding constraintsstructural

Long-term, flexible research support is shrinking. Traditional grants are short-cycle and siloed; they don't support the kind of open-ended, cross-disciplinary work that AI-for-science demands.

Unequal compute accessequity

Advanced AI infrastructure is concentrated in a handful of corporations and elite universities. Researchers globally face massive barriers to entry.

Closed research cultureaccess

Paywalled discoveries, proprietary datasets, and non-reproducible results slow the pace of collective progress.

Tool mismatchcomplexity

Scientific problems are increasingly data-rich and complex. Existing tools weren't designed for this scale. New infrastructure is needed, not incremental patches.

Commentary: The timing matters. Publication output in AI-for-science has surged ~15x since 2015 across Nature-indexed journals, yet this still represents under 3% of total research output. Meanwhile, compute costs fall ~30% annually. SAIR is betting that a well-governed foundation can channel these trends before corporate interests dominate the space entirely.

Pillar 1: AI for Science

Accelerate discovery

Using artificial intelligence to transform how scientific research is conducted — not just incremental improvement, but a fundamental shift in methodology.

Hypothesis generation

AI systems that scan literature, identify gaps, and propose testable hypotheses across domains — doing in hours what would take a human researcher months of reading.

Experiment design & simulation

Machine learning models that predict experimental outcomes, optimize parameters, and reduce the number of physical experiments needed. From drug discovery to materials science.

Neural networks processing datasets too large and complex for human analysis — genomic sequences, astronomical surveys, climate simulations, particle physics collision data.

Machine-assisted proof

Tao's specialty: formal proof assistants (like Lean), LLMs, and shared repositories as tools that help mathematicians verify and discover proofs. "Maturing pillars" of a new research paradigm.

Commentary: "AI for Science" isn't just about throwing LLMs at research papers. The strongest results so far come from domain-specific models: AlphaFold for protein structure, neural network potentials for molecular dynamics, ML-driven turbulence models. The challenge is building infrastructure that makes these tools accessible beyond the labs that created them.

Pillar 2: Science for AI

Ground intelligence

Applying the scientific method — hypothesis, experiment, verification, reproducibility — to the design, evaluation, and governance of AI systems themselves.

Rigorous evaluation standards

Benchmarks tied to actual research objectives, not just leaderboard metrics. Calibrated model evaluation that tells you when to trust an AI's output and when to be skeptical.

beyond benchmarkscalibration

Reproducibility protocols

Open datasets, standardized reporting, audit trails. If a result can't be reproduced, it's not science — and the same standard should apply to AI-generated findings.

open datareplication

Uncertainty quantification

AI models that know what they don't know. Incorporating prior knowledge, expressing confidence intervals, and flagging when predictions are outside their training distribution.

epistemic humilityBayesian

Evidence-based governance

AI policy driven by data and experiment, not intuition or fear. Risk-aligned frameworks that balance innovation with safety, transparency with competitiveness.

policygovernance
Commentary: This is the more provocative pillar. Most AI labs optimize for capability; SAIR is arguing that scientific methodology itself should constrain how AI is built and deployed. It's a bet that the scientific method — peer review, reproducibility, falsifiability — can serve as a governance framework for AI, not just a source of training data.

Three converging opportunities

where SAIR operates

AI + Science

AI is not just a tool for incremental improvement — it can change how we do science from hypothesis generation to experiment design to data interpretation. This requires new infrastructure, new funding modes, and new open frameworks.

Global Inclusion & Open Science

Creating equitable participation across geographies and institutions. Open datasets, shared compute, transparent standards, and community ownership — not a closed platform controlled by a few.

Ethical & Collaborative

Prioritizing transparency, data governance, reproducibility, and intellectual freedom. Authors retain ownership. Collaboration between academia, industry, and government without capture by any one.

Commentary: The "open science" emphasis is strategic positioning. With major AI labs increasingly proprietary and government funding uncertain, SAIR is carving out a space for publicly governed AI-science infrastructure. Whether this can compete with the resources of frontier labs (whose compute budgets dwarf any foundation's) remains the central tension.

Tao's keynote: machine assistance & the future of research mathematics

AI for Science: Kickoff 2026

At the SAIR launch, Tao outlined how AI tools are beginning to change the practice of mathematics — not by replacing human mathematicians, but by augmenting their capabilities across three "maturing pillars."

Formal proof assistants

Systems like Lean that can verify every step of a mathematical proof with computer-checkable certainty. Tao has used these in his own work, including a formalization of the PFR conjecture.

Large language models

LLMs as "literature review" tools and brainstorming partners — suggesting approaches, recalling techniques, and generating candidates for human verification. Not trusted alone, but valuable in a human-AI loop.

Shared repositories

Collaborative proof dashboards and open repositories that allow many mathematicians to work on interconnected problems simultaneously. A shift from solo work to "big science" mathematics.

"The way we've done mathematics has not changed fundamentally in centuries. We still work with pen and paper. Our collaborations are still very small — two, three people."
— Terence Tao
Commentary: Tao's Polymath project (a large-scale online math collaboration) was an early experiment in this direction. With formal verification + LLMs + shared infrastructure, the model could scale further: AI generates proof candidates, Lean verifies them, humans guide the overall strategy. SAIR is positioning itself to fund this pipeline.

The big picture: what SAIR is betting on

Thesis: open science wins

1) Science needs AI — but on its own terms

AI can transform research, but only if it's governed by scientific principles: reproducibility, peer review, uncertainty quantification. Without these guardrails, AI-generated science risks being fast but unreliable.

2) AI needs science — more than it admits

The scientific method is a proven framework for generating reliable knowledge under uncertainty. Applying it to AI development — hypothesis-driven design, falsifiable claims, calibrated evaluation — could address the field's reproducibility crisis.

3) Openness is a competitive advantage

Closed labs optimize for capability. Open ecosystems optimize for reliability and breadth. SAIR bets that in the long run, the open model produces more durable scientific progress — even if it's slower in the short term.

4) The window is now

Compute costs are falling ~30% annually. AI-for-science publications are surging. But infrastructure norms are still being set. Whoever builds the shared platforms now shapes the field for decades. SAIR is racing to be that builder.

The hardest question: can a foundation compete with corporations?click to expand

The elephant in the room: Google DeepMind's compute budget dwarfs what any foundation can offer. OpenAI's talent pool is formidable. Can SAIR's "open + governed" model actually compete?

The answer may lie in the CERN analogy. CERN doesn't compete with private companies — it provides infrastructure and coordination that no single company would build, because the returns are too diffuse and long-term. If SAIR can become the "CERN of AI-for-science" — providing shared datasets, benchmarks, compute access, and governance standards — it doesn't need to out-spend Google. It needs to be the neutral ground where everyone's work becomes more productive.
