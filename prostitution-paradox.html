1) The paradox in one sentence

If sex work persists regardless of law, then policy mainly decides who controls it (worker vs intermediaries), how visible it is, and how safe it is.

Moral institutions influence norms — but outcomes depend on policy details

Religious organizations (including churches) often oppose legalization to reduce vice or protect families. That stance isn’t automatically “pimping.” The question is: does the policy they support actually reduce harm, or does it shift harm underground?

Key idea You can argue “prostitution shouldn’t exist” morally, and still accept that policy must manage reality. The paradox is when a moral position unintentionally increases the power of exploiters by pushing the market into darkness.

4) The “paradox” distilled

A practical framing of your argument, sharpened but fair.
If the activity persists, law decides who holds the bargaining power. When illegal: secrecy becomes the scarce asset → intermediaries and coercive actors often gain leverage.
When regulated well: visibility and rights can shift power toward workers and reduce violence.
When regulated badly: a two-tier system forms → the safest are legal, the most vulnerable remain underground.
Where “the church” fits (without caricature) Churches and other moral institutions often influence policy through values and politics. The strongest critique isn’t “they are pimps,” but: does opposing legalization reduce harm, or does it unintentionally empower exploiters by pushing the market underground? If an institution wants to “protect,” the measurable test is support for harm reduction and exit pathways, not only prohibition.
